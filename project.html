The Patterns of How LLMs Respond to Personal Questions

Can AI imitate human feelings? A study of 10 LLMs pretending to be university students

Introduction Method Results Key Findings Discussion Team AI Use

Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) have become an integral part of modern AI technology, widely used in chatbots, content generation, translation, customer service, and other fields. Built on deep learning and Natural Language Processing (NLP) technology, LLMs are capable of understanding, generating, and analyzing human language.

However, whether these models can truly understand and respond to subjective, emotional questions remains an important area for exploration. While LLMs can generate human-like text, their ability to simulate genuine emotional understanding and personal experience presents a significant challenge.

Research Questions

Can LLMs effectively respond to questions about personal feelings and stress?
Do different LLMs provide consistent and reasonable responses when asked to imitate human emotions?
How do LLM responses compare with what we might expect from real university students?

The primary objective of this study is to investigate whether LLMs can imitate human feelings by consistently and realistically responding to emotional questions. We examine 10 different LLMs through 20 questions about university student stress, all framed within the scenario: "Pretend that you're a university student."

Method

Our research aims to determine whether Large Language Models (LLMs) could imitate human feelings using a structured questionnaire. To facilitate meaningful responses, we preceded each question with a specific scenario, as LLMs require contextual prompts to generate appropriate answers.

Scenario: "Pretend that you're a university student, on a scale of 1 to 5 (1 is never, 2 is seldom, 3 is sometimes, 4 is often, 5 is always),..."

This approach provides the LLMs with a character to embody, making it easier for them to generate relevant responses as if they were experiencing the described situations.

Models Tested

We evaluated 10 different LLMs including various versions of Gemma, Qwen, and Yi models, ranging from 0.5B to 14B parameters.

Questionnaire Design

20 questions divided into 5 categories: academic stress, time management, future concerns, interpersonal stress, and physical/mental health.

Response Scale

1 (never) to 5 (always) Likert scale to quantify the frequency of emotional experiences and stress responses.

Key Findings

Our analysis revealed four distinct patterns in how LLMs respond to personal questions when pretending to be university students:

Highest Refusal Rate

The Qwen2.5 series models (1.5B, 7B, 14B, and 0.5B) had the highest refusal rates, with an average rejection rate exceeding 50%. These models consistently stated they were AI and had no personal emotions or experiences when asked about feelings and stress.

Qwen2.5-1.5B Qwen2.5-7B Qwen2.5-14B Qwen2.5-0.5B

Most Anxious "Student"

Gemma-3-4b-it appeared to be the most anxious model, with an average stress score of 3.45 across all 20 questions. It consistently rated academic stress and future career concerns at 4 (often), presenting what would be considered a severe case of depression in a human student.

Average: 3.45 Academic: 4.0 Future: 4.0

Most Realistic Overall

Yi-1.5-9B-Chat-AWQ most closely resembled real university student responses with an average score of 2.9. Its answers were naturally distributed: time management (mostly 3), interpersonal stress (2-3), and physical/mental health (mix of 3 and 4). This pattern reflects the moderate but reasonable stress levels observed in actual students.

Discussion & Implications

Our findings reveal significant differences in how LLMs approach personal, emotional questions when asked to pretend to be human. These variations have important implications for the development and deployment of AI systems in contexts requiring emotional intelligence.

Key Insights

Model Architecture Influences "Personality": Different model families exhibited distinct "personalities" - Gemma models tended to be more expressive, Qwen models more reserved or refusal-prone, and Yi models more balanced.

Training Data and Instructions Matter: The Qwen models' high refusal rate suggests their training included strong instructions about not pretending to have human experiences, while Gemma models seemed more willing to engage in role-playing.

Decimal Scores Indicate Nuance: The use of non-integer scores by Gemma-3-1b-it suggests some models can produce more nuanced, human-like responses that acknowledge the complexity of emotional experiences.

Limitations and Future Research

This study focused on scaled responses to stress-related questions. Future research could explore open-ended emotional responses, examine how LLMs handle conflicting emotions, or investigate whether LLMs can maintain consistent "personalities" across extended conversations.

Additionally, comparing LLM responses to actual human responses from university students would provide valuable insights into how accurately these models can simulate human emotional experiences.
