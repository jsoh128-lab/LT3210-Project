Do LLMs Understand Emojis?

Investigating Emoji Comprehension in Large Language Models

Introduction Method Results References Team AI Declaration

Introduction

In today's digital communication landscape, emojis have become an integral part of how we express emotions, tone, and context in text-based conversations. As Large Language Models (LLMs) increasingly mediate human communication through chatbots, writing assistants, and other applications, it's crucial to understand how well these AI systems comprehend and interpret emojis.

This study investigates the capability of various LLMs to understand and appropriately respond to emojis in different contexts. We examine whether these models can accurately interpret emoji semantics, handle emoji combinations, and maintain contextual coherence when emojis are included in prompts.

Research Questions

1. How accurately do LLMs interpret the semantic meaning of individual emojis?
2. Can LLMs correctly understand emoji combinations and sequences?
3. How do LLMs handle emojis in contextually complex scenarios?

Method

We designed a comprehensive questionnaire to evaluate emoji understanding across multiple LLMs. The questionnaire included various types of prompts:

Single Emoji Interpretation

Models were asked to explain the meaning of individual emojis in different contexts.

Emoji Combinations

We tested how models interpret sequences of emojis and whether they can identify emergent meanings.

Contextual Understanding

Models received text prompts with embedded emojis and were evaluated on their ability to maintain contextual coherence.

Results and Discussion

Our evaluation of four popular LLMs revealed significant variations in emoji comprehension capabilities.

Discussion

Our findings indicate that while modern LLMs have developed reasonable emoji comprehension, there are notable limitations. Models performed best with common, frequently used emojis but struggled with less common or culturally specific emojis. Additionally, emoji combinations presented challenges, suggesting that models may not fully grasp emergent meanings from sequences.

The contextual analysis revealed that models generally maintain coherence when emojis supplement text, but performance degraded when emojis replaced key textual elements. This suggests that current LLMs treat emojis primarily as supplementary rather than primary carriers of meaning.

These results have implications for the development of more emoji-aware AI systems, particularly as digital communication continues to evolve toward more visual and symbolic expression.
